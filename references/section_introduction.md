# Introduction

## Purpose

Lead the reader from a shared research context to the specific need for the present study.

Use the move codes as diagnostic labels, not rigid sentence templates.

## I1. Establish context and significance

Possible functions:

- establish why the field or problem matters;
- provide shared background;
- define a key term;
- identify the broad problem or current research focus.

Start at a level appropriate to the audience. Avoid generic claims that could introduce almost any paper. A claim of importance should name the actual scientific, practical, or methodological reason.

## I2. Synthesize relevant prior research

Do more than list papers.

Organize literature by:

- chronology when development matters;
- approach, model, mechanism, or method;
- agreement and disagreement;
- scale, material, population, or condition;
- progression toward the current question.

Use past simple for specific completed studies and present perfect for a body of work with current relevance. Present simple may be used for findings treated as established.

Keep citation ownership clear.

## I3. Identify the gap, problem, question, or hypothesis

A gap may be:

- missing evidence;
- unresolved disagreement;
- untested condition or scale;
- methodological limitation;
- poor transferability;
- unclear mechanism;
- inconsistent results;
- lack of comparison or validation.

Do not manufacture a `little is known` gap when substantial literature exists.

Connect the gap directly to the study's question.

## I4. Present the current study

State as appropriate:

- objective or research question;
- hypothesis or prediction;
- approach or design;
- scope and key conditions;
- paper organization when useful;
- main result if normal for the journal.

Prefer an explicit objective to a vague statement that the topic is `discussed`.

## Funnel check

A defensible Introduction normally narrows:

```text
field/problem -> relevant research -> unresolved need -> present study
```

Each paragraph should reduce uncertainty about why the study was necessary.

## Common failures

- opening with the exact aim before readers understand the context;
- asserting importance without a concrete reason;
- paper-by-paper literature summaries with no synthesis;
- a gap unrelated to the actual method or results;
- unsupported novelty;
- mixing detailed Results or Discussion into background;
- ending without a clear objective;
- promising analyses or mechanisms not delivered later.

## Audit

Label paragraphs I1-I4, allowing combinations. Ask:

- Is significance established rather than asserted?
- Is the literature map selective and relevant?
- Is the transition from prior work to gap explicit?
- Does the gap match the research question?
- Does the present-study statement match Methods and Results?
- Are tense shifts meaningful?
- Are citations attached to the right claims?
