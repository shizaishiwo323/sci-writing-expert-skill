# Methods / Materials and Methods

## Purpose

Show how the research question was made answerable, reproducible, and credible.

The section is an evidence chain, not merely an equipment inventory.

## M1. Orient the reader

Possible functions:

- give a short design or workflow overview;
- restate the methodological purpose;
- identify materials, equipment, datasets, sites, or participants;
- provide essential setup information.

Do not repeat the full Introduction.

## M2. Provide reproducible details and justification

Report as relevant:

- quantities, dimensions, units, concentrations, temperatures, durations;
- sequence and timing;
- inclusion/exclusion criteria;
- sampling and randomization;
- boundary and initial conditions;
- instrument settings and calibration;
- preprocessing and quality control;
- equations, algorithms, software versions, and parameters;
- statistical tests and thresholds;
- ethical approval and consent;
- reasons for non-obvious choices.

Give enough detail for replication or informed evaluation.

## M3. Relate the procedure to established methods

Distinguish among:

- used as published;
- adapted;
- modified;
- extended;
- newly developed.

State what changed and why when the change affects reproducibility or interpretation. Attach citations to the exact borrowed method.

## M4. Report constraints or procedural problems

Mention issues that affect:

- sample representativeness;
- measurement accuracy;
- missing data;
- instrument limits;
- convergence;
- contamination;
- protocol deviations;
- uncertainty.

State their likely impact without exaggeration.

## Tense and voice

Typical defaults:

- study-specific actions: past simple;
- standard procedures, equations, or permanent setup descriptions: present simple;
- active voice for analytical decisions and author contributions;
- passive voice when process or object is the focus.

Make the boundary between current work and cited work unmistakable.

## Sequence and modifiers

Use explicit sequence when order affects reproducibility. Put temporal, spatial, and instrumental modifiers next to the step they qualify.

Avoid ambiguous forms such as:

```text
Samples were analyzed after heating using the instrument.
```

when it is unclear what was heated.

## Evidence-chain audit

For each major result:

```text
research question
-> design choice
-> measurement or computation
-> quality control
-> analysis
-> uncertainty
```

A result with no visible methodological support is a reviewer risk.

## Common failures

- actions listed without a design overview;
- missing parameter values or sample counts;
- unexplained method choice;
- unclear material/data source;
- procedural results mixed into Methods;
- unclear standard versus study-specific procedure;
- excessive passive voice that hides decisions;
- `as described previously` without enough local detail;
- software named without version/settings when these matter.
