# Style Principles

## Core target

A strong scientific sentence is clear, concrete, restrained, and easy to verify. Readers should quickly identify:

- the object, process, model, signal, or metric;
- what changed, was measured, compared, or inferred;
- evidence strength;
- the conditions and scope that bound the claim.

Good editing usually makes prose simpler, not more ornate.

## 1. Sentence architecture

### Use a concrete subject

Move the actual actor or scientific object into subject position.

Prefer:

- `The model predicts...`
- `The spectrum shifts...`
- `Porosity increases...`
- `We compared...`

Question long abstract openings such as `The establishment of a comprehensive...`.

### Bring the main verb early

Move long definitions, conditions, and secondary modifiers after the core subject-verb pair when possible.

Do not remove constraints merely to shorten the opening.

### Reduce abstract noun stacks

When meaning is preserved:

- `the implementation of segmentation` -> `segmenting`;
- `the occurrence of dissolution` -> `dissolution`;
- `the enhancement of permeability` -> `permeability increased`.

Keep standard technical noun phrases.

## 2. Verb choice

Prefer direct, ordinary scientific verbs:

- `show`, `indicate`, `suggest`;
- `define`, `measure`, `estimate`;
- `compare`, `link`, `track`;
- `increase`, `decrease`, `shift`, `merge`, `separate`.

Use cautiously:

- `demonstrate`, `establish`, `reveal`, `control`, `ensure`.

Question decorative or promotional verbs:

- `elucidate`, `unveil`, `leverage`, `revolutionize`.

Do not replace a precise disciplinary verb merely because it is uncommon.

## 3. Observation before interpretation

When a result sentence contains both:

```text
measured observation -> process/interpretation -> bounded implication
```

State the evidence before the explanatory claim unless the paragraph's logic clearly requires another order.

Do not add a mechanism to make the sentence sound deeper.

## 4. Preserve comparison baselines

Keep:

- reference case;
- changed and unchanged quantities;
- direction;
- magnitude;
- condition;
- denominator.

Do not smooth away a comparator that controls the interpretation.

## 5. Keep implications local

Preserve scope markers:

- `in this system`;
- `under these conditions`;
- `for the tested cases`;
- `in the simulations`;
- `within the measured range`.

Do not move from one simulation or experiment to a universal or field-scale conclusion.

## 6. Remove decorative intensifiers

Usually delete or replace with data:

- `critically`;
- `remarkably`;
- `fundamentally`;
- `dramatically`;
- `profoundly`;
- `unprecedented`;
- `robustly`;
- `significantly` when no statistical meaning is intended.

Prefer exact numbers or neutral trend verbs.

## 7. Plain academic vocabulary

Use simpler wording when equally precise:

| Wordy/inflated | Often clearer |
|---|---|
| utilize | use |
| leverage | use |
| elucidate | show, explain |
| delineate | define, distinguish |
| facilitate | support, help, allow |
| manifest as | appear as, produce |
| prior to | before |
| subsequent to | after |
| in order to | to |
| owing to the fact that | because |

Do not simplify standard technical terminology.

## 8. Reader guidance

Use transitions, topic sentences, and controlled repetition to reveal the logic. Do not fill the paper with formulaic signposting.

A paragraph should not require readers to infer whether the next sentence is a cause, result, contrast, example, or limitation.
