# Claim Strength, Causality, and Modality

## 1. Identify the evidence type

Does the evidence support:

- description;
- difference;
- association;
- temporal ordering;
- partial contribution;
- causation;
- mechanism;
- prediction;
- generalization;
- recommendation?

Do not express a stronger relation than the design supports.

## 2. Reporting-verb ladder

Approximate and context-dependent:

```text
proves / establishes
demonstrates / shows
indicates
supports / is consistent with
suggests
may suggest / could indicate
is compatible with / cannot exclude
```

`Shows` may be neutral for a figure but strong for a mechanism. Judge the object.

## 3. Causal language

### Strong causation

Use only when justified:

- `caused`;
- `resulted in`;
- `led to`;
- `was due to`;
- `produced`;
- `triggered`.

### Partial contribution

- `contributed to`;
- `was a factor in`;
- `partly accounted for`;
- `influenced`.

### Association or uncertain direction

- `was associated with`;
- `was related to`;
- `correlated with`;
- `coincided with`;
- `was linked to`.

Do not use association language and then present the mechanism as settled.

### Direction check

- `X resulted from Y`: Y caused X.
- `X resulted in Y`: X caused Y.
- `a cause`: one possible cause.
- `the cause`: sole or uniquely identified cause.

## 4. Modal meanings

### Possibility

- `may`, `might`, `could`;
- past: `may/might/could have`.

### Capability

- `can`;
- `could` for general past capability;
- `was/were able to` for a specific successful past event when appropriate.

### Expectation or probability

- `should`, `ought to`;
- distinguish from advice.

### Deductive near-certainty without direct proof

- `must`, `cannot`;
- `must` often signals inference, not direct observation.

### Necessity

- `must`, `need to`, `have to`;
- `must not` means prohibition;
- `does not need to` or `need not` means no necessity.

## 5. Hedge placement

Attach the hedge to the exact proposition being limited.

Avoid stacking:

```text
The results may possibly appear to suggest that...
```

Choose one calibrated form.

## 6. Scope control

Retain restrictions such as:

- `under these conditions`;
- `in the present simulations`;
- `for the tested samples`;
- `within the measured range`;
- `at the laboratory scale`;
- `in this cohort`.

## 7. Novelty and importance

Use `new`, `novel`, `for the first time`, or `unprecedented` only after verification.

Prefer a concrete contribution:

```text
The study provides measurements of X across Y conditions.
```

not unsupported promotion.

## 8. Statistical and practical claims

Distinguish:

- statistically significant;
- numerically large;
- scientifically meaningful;
- clinically or practically relevant.

A statistically significant effect may be small; a large effect may be uncertain.

## 9. Calibration check

- What evidence licenses this verb?
- Does the sentence imply one cause or several?
- Is the claim local or general?
- Is uncertainty attached to the correct proposition?
- Was any condition removed?
- Did a modal change meaning in negative or past form?
