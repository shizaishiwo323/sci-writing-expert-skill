# Abstract and Title

## Abstract purpose

Provide a self-contained, compressed representation of the paper that lets the right reader decide whether to read it.

Obey journal word count, headings, and article-type rules before generic guidance.

## Abstract move set

### A1. Context, problem, or gap

Use one or two concise sentences only when background is needed.

### A2. Aim or paper action

State the objective, question, hypothesis, or what the paper does.

### A3. Methods or materials

Identify the design, data, model, experiment, or analysis needed to evaluate the result. Include critical conditions or sample size when they determine relevance.

### A4. Key results, contribution, and bounded implication

This is usually the highest-information part.

- report the main result;
- include decisive quantitative values where useful;
- identify the contribution;
- state an implication at the strength supported by the paper.

Avoid `better performance` without a comparator or metric.

### A5. Application, limitation, or future work

Include only when central and allowed.

## Common patterns

### Structured or expanded

```text
context/problem -> aim -> methods -> results -> conclusion/implication
```

### Compressed

```text
paper action + aim + method
key result + quantitative detail + contribution
bounded implication
```

Methods normally precede Results even when compressed.

## Tense

- context/current problem: present simple;
- aim: present or past by journal convention;
- methods: past simple;
- study results: past simple;
- general relation: present simple when justified;
- current contribution/application: present simple or present perfect.

## Abstract checks

- understandable without the main text;
- abbreviations handled by journal rules;
- main method and result identifiable;
- key search terms present naturally;
- no promises such as `will be discussed`;
- numbers and claim strength match the paper;
- citations omitted unless genuinely required;
- word count compliant.

## Title purpose

Predict the paper accurately and make it retrievable.

## Title rules

A strong title is:

- specific about system, process, method, or population;
- concise but not cryptic;
- rich in meaningful search terms;
- neutral in value;
- clear about case-study versus general scope;
- in journal-required capitalization.

Avoid:

- `A study of` when removable;
- overlong noun stacks;
- ambiguous prepositions;
- unexplained abbreviations;
- unsupported `novel`, `excellent`, or `reliable`;
- modal claims;
- broader scope than the study;
- question titles unless normal for the journal.

## Title workflow

1. State the research question in plain language.
2. Identify variables, relation, system, and scope.
3. Select a pattern:
   - effect of X on Y under Z;
   - comparison of X and Y for Z;
   - method X for measuring/predicting Y;
   - mechanism/relationship statement;
   - supported result statement;
   - case study with location/population.
4. Remove empty words.
5. Check noun-stack and preposition ambiguity.
6. Verify every claim against the paper.

## Output

For Abstract:

```text
Revised abstract:
...

Word count:
...

Main structural changes:
- ...
```

For titles, provide 3-6 options grouped by pattern only when useful, and label stronger result-oriented options.
