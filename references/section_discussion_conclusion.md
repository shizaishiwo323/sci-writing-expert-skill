# Discussion and Conclusion

## Purpose

Explain what the findings mean, how they change or refine existing understanding, and where the claims stop.

Discussion generally widens from specific findings toward broader meaning.

## D1. Revisit the question and synthesize key findings

Begin with the answer or most important finding, not a complete repetition of Results.

Synthesize:

- the central pattern;
- the tested conditions;
- which result matters most;
- how it answers the original question.

## D2. Map findings to existing research

Show whether the findings:

- agree with;
- extend;
- refine;
- qualify;
- contradict;
- reconcile;
- explain;
- fail to reproduce

prior work.

A comparison needs a basis: method, condition, scale, material, population, or theory. Do not label findings inconsistent when studies are not comparable.

## D3. Explain contribution and implications

Possible contributions:

- new evidence;
- improved measurement or method;
- extension to a new condition or scale;
- mechanism clarification;
- resolution of a disagreement;
- model validation or falsification;
- practical capability.

Derive implications from Results and keep mechanism claims calibrated.

## D4. State limitations, future work, and applications

A useful limitation states:

```text
constraint -> likely effect on interpretation -> bounded consequence
```

Future work should follow from a real limitation or unresolved question. Avoid generic `more research is needed`.

Applications should be plausible at the demonstrated scale. Separate immediate use from long-term potential.

## Logic patterns

```text
key finding -> explanation -> literature comparison -> implication
unexpected finding -> possible explanations -> evidence assessment -> next test
agreement -> extension -> boundary condition
limitation -> effect on claim -> mitigation/future work
```

## Modal and causal discipline

Do not use `must` merely as a stronger `shows`; it often signals deduction without direct proof.

Do not convert correlation into mechanism.

## Conclusion

A Conclusion may:

- restate the answer at a higher level;
- identify the main contribution;
- state the most defensible implication;
- mention a central limitation or next step.

It should not:

- introduce new evidence;
- copy the Abstract;
- repeat every result;
- end with generic importance language;
- claim universal applicability from a bounded study.

## Common failures

- re-narrating figures rather than interpreting them;
- discussing an untested mechanism;
- literature comparison without explaining differences;
- unsupported novelty or importance;
- ritual limitations detached from claims;
- future work unrelated to actual uncertainty;
- applications beyond the tested scale;
- contradictory claim strength across sections.

## Audit

Map paragraphs D1-D4 and ask:

- Is the central answer visible early?
- Does each interpretation point to evidence?
- Are alternatives considered where necessary?
- Is literature agreement/disagreement based on comparable conditions?
- Does contribution match the Introduction's gap?
- Are limitations linked to claim boundaries?
- Does the ending close the paper?
