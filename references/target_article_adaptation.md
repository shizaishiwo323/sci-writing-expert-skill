# Target-Article Adaptation

Generic scientific-writing rules are defaults. A representative target corpus reveals field and journal conventions.

## 1. Preferred corpus

Use 3-5 recent research articles that:

- come from the target journal or a close substitute;
- use the same article type;
- employ related methods and address a similar audience;
- have identifiable sections;
- are not review articles when the user is writing original research.

Do not infer a journal's style from one unusual paper.

## 2. Extract patterns, not wording

Inspect:

- section names and order;
- Introduction length and move sequence;
- Methods tense and voice;
- first-person versus passive usage;
- separate or combined Results/Discussion;
- paragraph length and topic-sentence style;
- figure-callout conventions;
- abstract type, word count, and tense pattern;
- title structure and capitalization;
- reporting verbs and claim-strength patterns.

Learn reusable syntax and rhetorical functions. Never copy distinctive sentences.

## 3. Build a journal profile

```text
Journal/article type:
Audience:
Section structure:
Preferred authorial voice:
Methods tense:
Results tense:
Results-Discussion boundary:
Abstract pattern:
Title pattern:
Notable restrictions:
```

## 4. Resolve conflicts

Priority:

1. explicit journal instructions;
2. stable pattern across the target corpus;
3. the user's established terminology;
4. generic rules in this skill.

Do not imitate an error merely because one paper contains it.

## 5. Report adaptation decisions

Distinguish:

- **required correction:** grammar, ambiguity, evidence error;
- **journal adaptation:** a convention supported by the corpus;
- **optional preference:** variation with no material benefit.

A small corpus supports a practical profile, not a claim of statistical representativeness.
