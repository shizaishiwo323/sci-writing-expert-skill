# Results

## Purpose

Present the evidence needed to answer the research question in an order that reveals the pattern and permits evaluation.

## R1. Orient the reader

Possible functions:

- briefly revisit the aim or analysis;
- remind the reader of a relevant method;
- preview the result sequence;
- state the main pattern before details.

Do not repeat the entire Methods section.

## R2. Present key evidence

A strong result unit often follows:

```text
result claim -> quantitative/visual evidence -> comparison -> local explanation
```

Use figures and tables as evidence, not a script for describing every visible value.

When referring to a visual:

- identify the relevant panel or variable;
- state the pattern readers should notice;
- give critical values, uncertainty, or comparisons;
- avoid empty descriptions such as `Figure 2 is about...`.

Explain why a value is large, small, similar, or unexpected relative to an explicit reference.

## R3. Report anomalies, uncertainty, or imperfect results

Include:

- null or negative findings;
- outliers or deviations;
- uncertainty and variability;
- model-data mismatch;
- conditions under which the pattern changes.

Do not erase inconvenient evidence. State whether it materially affects the main interpretation.

## R4. Give a bounded implication

Depending on journal convention, Results may end with a local interpretation or implication.

Keep it close to the evidence. Reserve broad literature integration, general significance, limitations, and applications for Discussion.

## Ordering principles

Organize by:

- research question or hypothesis;
- experimental sequence;
- variable or regime;
- spatial or temporal scale;
- primary to secondary outcome;
- observation to mechanism;
- logical figure order rather than merely numerical order.

## Tense

- past simple: study-specific observations;
- present simple: what a figure shows, mathematical relations, or a justified general relation.

Do not use present tense merely to strengthen a weak finding.

## Results versus Discussion

Immediate interpretation is acceptable when journal convention allows it, but:

- observation and explanation must remain distinguishable;
- literature comparison should not overwhelm the result;
- speculative mechanisms must be signaled;
- broad implications should wait.

## Common failures

- panel-by-panel description without a claim;
- repeating table values without interpretation;
- `significant` with no statistical meaning;
- switching comparator or denominator;
- hiding null findings;
- mixing methods, results, and broad discussion in one paragraph;
- causal wording from observational evidence;
- order that does not answer the research questions;
- beginning every sentence with `Figure X shows`.

## Audit

For each paragraph:

```text
question addressed:
main result:
evidence:
comparison:
uncertainty:
local implication:
```

If the main result cannot be stated in one sentence, the paragraph may lack focus.
