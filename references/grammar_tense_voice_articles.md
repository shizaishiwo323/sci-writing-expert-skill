# Grammar for Scientific Writing

## 1. Tense as rhetorical meaning

Do not normalize every verb to one tense. Tense positions information.

| Function | Common default | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| accepted fact/current generalization | present simple | treated as currently valid |
| research area with current relevance | present perfect | past activity matters now |
| specific completed cited study | past simple | bounded past event |
| standard procedure/equation | present simple | conventional/general |
| procedure performed in this study | past simple | completed study action |
| result observed in this study | past simple | bounded finding |
| result framed as general relation | present simple | stronger/generalized status |
| present paper's action | present simple | `This paper examines...` |

A tense shift should correspond to a functional shift.

### Introduction

Typical pattern:

```text
present simple: established context
present perfect: current research state or unresolved gap
past simple: named completed studies
present simple: what the paper does
```

### Methods

Distinguish:

- standard procedure: `X is measured using...`
- this study: `X was measured using...`

If the field uses present tense for current procedures, mark agency with `In this study` or `we`.

### Results

Past simple is the conservative default for observations. Present simple may suit:

- a figure/table that currently displays information;
- a mathematical relation;
- a finding explicitly framed as generally valid.

Do not use present tense merely to make a result sound stronger.

### Abstract

Tenses may mix:

- background/problem: present simple;
- aim: present or past by journal convention;
- methods: past simple;
- results: past or present according to claim status;
- current contribution/application: present simple or present perfect.

## 2. Active and passive voice

Use active voice when it clarifies:

- who made a decision;
- what the authors contributed;
- which study produced a result;
- the actor-action relation.

Use passive voice when:

- the process or object is the information focus;
- the actor is obvious and irrelevant;
- field/journal convention favors it;
- procedural description is clearer.

Avoid agentless passive when it obscures current versus cited work.

Acceptable subjects include `we`, `this study`, `the analysis`, `the model`, `the experiment`, `the results`, and `Figure 2`.

Do not use `we` to mean people in general.

## 3. Articles and determiners

### Singular count nouns

A singular count noun normally needs a determiner:

- `a method`;
- `the method`;
- `this method`;
- `each method`.

### `a/an`

Use for a newly introduced, non-unique, or one-of-several referent.

### `the`

Use for a shared, previously introduced, defined, unique, or identifiable referent.

The choice can change meaning:

- `a relationship`: some relationship;
- `the relationship`: a specific relationship.

### Zero article

Common with plural or uncountable nouns used generically and with many names, subject to field convention.

Check countability of `analysis`, `research`, `evidence`, `equipment`, `behavior`, and `work`.

Use `a/an` by sound, not spelling.

## 4. Modifier placement

Place a modifier next to its target.

High-risk words:

- `only`;
- `also`;
- `respectively`;
- `approximately`;
- `significantly`;
- `initially`;
- `subsequently`;
- `using`;
- `based on`.

Rewrite a participial opener if its subject is unclear.

## 5. Sequence

Sequence language can mark:

- prior state;
- start;
- next step;
- short delay;
- later stage;
- simultaneous event;
- final stage;
- post-experiment action.

Use explicit timing where reproducibility depends on it; do not rely on repeated `then`.

## 6. Frequency

Frequency terms are interpretive:

```text
always / consistently
usually / generally
often / frequently
sometimes
occasionally
rarely
never
```

Use exact counts or proportions when reproducibility or comparison requires them.

## 7. Quantity

Text should interpret important values, not merely repeat a table.

Distinguish:

- magnitude: `substantial`, `small`, `negligible`;
- comparison: `higher`, `lower`, `similar`;
- proximity: `approximately`, `nearly`;
- emphasis: `as high as`, `only`;
- neutral quantity: `some`, `several`.

Each evaluative quantity word needs a reference point. `Only 20%` and `as much as 20%` frame the same number differently.

Use `significant` for statistical significance only when that is intended; otherwise use a direct numerical description or a suitable magnitude word.
