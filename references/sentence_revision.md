# Sentence Revision

## Objective

Make each sentence clear, conventional, and easy to process without changing scientific content.

## 1. Identify the sentence's job

A sentence may:

- establish context;
- report prior research;
- identify a gap;
- state an aim or hypothesis;
- describe a method;
- report or compare results;
- interpret evidence;
- qualify a claim;
- state a limitation or implication;
- direct the reader to a figure, table, or equation.

Grammar and emphasis should support that job.

## 2. Revision priorities

### A. Expose the main actor and action

Keep the main verb close to the subject. Replace a long abstract subject with the actual process, object, model, or authors when appropriate.

### B. Prefer direct verbs

Consider:

- `analyzed` rather than `performed an analysis of`;
- `increased` rather than `showed an increase in`;
- `compared` rather than `made a comparison between`;
- `because` rather than `due to the fact that`.

Retain nominalizations that are established technical concepts.

### C. Control information load

Split when:

- two claims need different evidence or citations;
- a modifier can attach to more than one phrase;
- the sentence shifts from observation to interpretation;
- several independent logical turns compete;
- the reader must hold many conditions before the main verb.

Do not split tightly integrated definitions or mathematical statements merely to shorten them.

### D. Place modifiers next to targets

Check:

- `only`, `also`, `respectively`;
- `approximately`, `significantly`, `mainly`;
- participial and prepositional phrases;
- temporal, spatial, and instrumental conditions;
- figure/table qualifiers.

Rewrite dangling or ambiguously attached modifiers.

### E. Preserve parallelism

Coordinate comparable grammatical forms:

```text
The method reduces noise, improves stability, and preserves temporal resolution.
```

### F. Use connectors by actual relation

- cause: `because`, `as a result of`;
- result: `therefore`, `consequently`;
- contrast: `whereas`, `however`;
- concession: `although`, `nevertheless`;
- addition: `in addition`, `also`;
- example: `for example`;
- reformulation: `in other words`;
- sequence: `subsequently`, `after`, `during`.

A connector cannot repair unrelated ideas.

### G. Avoid inflated or vague wording

Question:

- `groundbreaking`, `remarkable`, `revolutionary`;
- `plays a crucial role` without the role;
- `provides valuable insights` without the insight;
- `comprehensive understanding` for a bounded analysis;
- decorative verbs where `shows`, `identifies`, or `explains` is more accurate.

## 3. Edit intensity

- **Light:** grammar, articles, punctuation, collocation, obvious awkwardness.
- **Standard:** clause order, concision, topic-to-comment flow, split/merge.
- **Deep:** reconstruct from the scientific proposition when syntax blocks meaning.

## 4. Verification

Ask:

- Can the reader find the subject and main verb quickly?
- Is agency clear?
- Is observation separated from interpretation when needed?
- Are comparison members scientifically comparable?
- Are pronoun referents clear?
- Does each citation support the intended claim?
- Are tense and modality intentional?
- Could a modifier attach to the wrong phrase?
- Does the ending prepare the next sentence?
